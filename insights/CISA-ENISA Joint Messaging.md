# CISA-ENISA Joint Messaging

## Executive Summary
Lindsey Cerkovnik (CISA, US) and Nuno Rodrigues Carvalho (ENISA, BE) highlight a critical strategic shift in the CVE program, marking the transition from a decade-long "growth era" to a new "quality era." This joint session emphasizes the importance of transatlantic cooperation in managing the global surge of vulnerability reports, accelerated by AI and emerging technologies. They outline a united vision for the program that prioritizes internationalization, infrastructure modernization, and technical interoperability, ensuring that CVE data remains a trusted foundation for global cybersecurity defense.

## Key Points
- The CVE program is moving beyond simple scaling (currently over 500 CNAs across 41 countries) to focus on "programmatic quality," which encompasses both data accuracy and the overall user experience for researchers.
- CISA remains the primary federal sponsor of CVE, treating the program as a top priority for "protecting life and property" despite operational challenges like federal shutdowns.
- ENISA has significantly expanded its role, advancing from a CNA to a "Root CNA" as of November 2023, with aspirations to become a "Top-Level Root" hub for the European Union.
- The anticipated "flood" of AI-generated vulnerability reports (referred to as "slop" when low-value) necessitates the development of more scalable, automated triage and disclosure frameworks.
- "Interoperability" is a mandatory goal: ENISA’s regional initiatives (like the EU vulnerability database) are designed to complement and align with the global CVE backbone rather than fragment the ecosystem.
- CISA’s "Vulnerability Enrichment" project serves as a testing ground to determine which specific metadata fields are most actionable for defenders and worth long-term investment.
- Infrastructure modernization is underway to simplify the CVE submission process, aiming to make it "pain-free" and efficient for a broader range of international partners.
- Program diversification is a high priority, with explicit calls for more participation from AI organizations and the open-source software community.

## Notable Quotes
- "We’ve been in a growth era for the last decade. We’re moving into a quality era."
- "The CVE program isn’t a US-centric program because this isn’t a US-centric problem."
- "Quality doesn’t actually just mean data quality... it means quality of experience as well."
- "Interoperability isn’t a question of should we; it’s a question of how do we do this."
- "Vulnerability management is often not very glamorous... we’re always telling people they need to do something they don’t want to do."

## Takeaways
- Prepare for increased scrutiny on CVE data quality as the program implements more rigorous standards for CNAs.
- EU-based organizations and national CSIRTs should leverage ENISA as their regional hub for CVE onboarding and support.
- Actively participate in CVE Working Groups (specifically the Researcher and Quality groups) to influence the modernization of the program's infrastructure.
- Monitor CISA's "Vulnerability Enrichment" outputs as a leading indicator of the types of metadata that will become standard in future CVE records.
